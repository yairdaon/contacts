\section*{Methods}
Our goal is to find a lower bound for estimation of a connectivity
parameter between regions. Our strategy is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Formulate a discrete SIR model for regional connectivity in
  terms of a connectivity parameter $\theta \in[0,0.5]$ (Section
  \ref{subsec:model}).
\item Calculate sensitivities for this model, i.e.~the derivatives of
  model output with respect to initial conditions and $\theta$
  (Section \ref{subsec:sens}).
\item Derive an observation model based on a constant reporting rate
  and approximate it as multiplicative Gaussian noise (Section
  \ref{subsec:noise}).
\item Find a maximum-likelihood estimate for $\theta$ and the initial
  conditions over several seasons (Section \ref{subsec:mle}).
\item Calculate a Cramer-Rao lower bound for the variance of the
  maximum-likelihood estimate of the connectivity parameter $\theta$
  (Section \ref{subsec:crlb}).
\end{enumerate}
We apply this approach to estimate connectivity $\theta$ between US
states and find accompanying a lower bound on the variance of the
estimator.


\subsection{Discrete-Time Connectivity Model}\label{subsec:model}
Consider $2$ interconnected regions, where $S_i(t)$, $I_i(t)$, and
$R_i(t)$ denote the fractions of Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered
individuals in region $i$ at time $t$.

We denote $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$ the contact matrix
with entries $C_{ij} \in [0,1]$ represents relative connectivity from
region $j$ to region $i$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact}
\mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 1-\theta & \theta \\ \theta & 1-\theta \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\theta \in [0, 0.5]$ is the connectivity parameter representing
the fraction of contacts that occur between regions. When $\theta = 0$,
the regions are isolated; when $\theta = 0.5$, contacts are equally
distributed within and between regions. Row sums equal unity, reflecting
frequency-dependent transmission.

We also take $\beta_i(t)$ is the region-specific
time-varying transmission rate with seasonal forcing:
\begin{equation*}
\beta_i(t) = \beta_0\left(1 + \delta\sin\left(2\pi t + \varphi_i\right)\right)
\end{equation*}
where $\beta_0$ is the baseline transmission rate, $\delta$ is the
seasonal amplitude, and $\varphi_i$ is the phase for region $i$.

We thus define the force of infection in region $i$ at time $t$ as:
\begin{equation*}
\lambda_i(t) = \beta_i(t) \sum_{j=1}^n C_{ij} I_j(t).
\end{equation*}

Over one time step, the probability that a susceptible individual in
region $i$ does \emph{not} become infected is $\exp(-\lambda_i(t))$,
yielding the infection probability $p_i(t) = 1 - \exp(-\lambda_i(t))$.
The incidence in region $i$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:incidence}
\mu_i(t) = S_i(t) \left[1 - \exp(-\lambda_i(t))\right]
\end{equation}

Similarly, the probability an infected individual remains infected
over one time step is $\exp(-\gamma)$, where $\gamma$ is the recovery
rate. The discrete-time SIR dynamics are thus:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:discrete_sir}
S_i(t+1) &= S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t)) \\
I_i(t+1) &= I_i(t) \exp(-\gamma) + \mu_i(t) \\
R_i(t+1) &= R_i(t) + I_i(t) [1 - \exp(-\gamma)]
\end{split}\end{align}

\subsection{Sensitivity equations}\label{subsec:sens}
Computing the Cram\'er-Rao bound and optimizing the likelihood both
require derivatives of model outputs with respect to parameters. Let
$\phi \in \{\theta, S_1(0), S_2(0), I_1(0), I_2(0)\}$ denote an
arbitrary parameter. We derive the sensitivities by differentiating
the discrete dynamics. We denote:
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{S}(t)&=\left (S_1(t), S_2(t)\right )\\
  \mathbf{I}(t)&=\left (I_1(t), I_2(t)\right )
\end{align*}
the vectors of Susceptibles and Infecteds; $\mathbf{C}$ still denotes
the connectivity matrix.

The sensitivity of the force of infection is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_lambda}
\frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi} = \beta_i(t) \left(
\frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \phi} \mathbf{I}(t) + \mathbf{C}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{I}(t)}{\partial \phi} \right)_i.
\end{equation}
Note that:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \theta} =
\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
while $\partial \mathbf{C}/\partial \phi = \mathbf{0}$ for $\phi \neq
\theta$.

The sensitivity of the expected incidence follows from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:incidence} by the product rule:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_mu}
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi} =
\frac{\partial S_i(t)}{\partial \phi} \left[1 - \exp(-\lambda_i(t))\right]
+ S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t)) \frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi}.
\end{equation}

The state sensitivities propagate according to:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:sens}
\frac{\partial S_i(t+1)}{\partial \phi} &= \exp(-\lambda_i(t))
\frac{\partial S_i(t)}{\partial \phi} - S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t))
\frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi} \\
\frac{\partial I_i(t+1)}{\partial \phi} &= \exp(-\gamma)
\frac{\partial I_i(t)}{\partial \phi} +
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\end{split}\end{align}

Initial conditions for the sensitivities are: $\partial
S_i(0)/\partial S_j(0) = \delta_{ij}$, $\partial I_i(0)/\partial
I_j(0) = \delta_{ij}$, and zero otherwise.  These equations are solved
jointly with the state equations in a single forward pass.

\subsection{The Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound}\label{subsec:crlb}
For a vetor of unbiased estimators the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound states
that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crlb_vector}
\text{Var}(\hat{\phi}_k) \geq [\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{kk}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{J}$ is the $p \times p$ \emph{Fisher Information
Matrix} with entries
$$
J_{kl} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_k}
  \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_l}\right].
$$
%
It is best to think of $\mathbf{J}$ as statistical precision, so
$\mathbf{J}^{-1}$ is a covariance matrix and (e.g.) its $(1,1)$ entry
is the variance of the estimator of $\phi_1$.

%% The CRLB has a key implication: if $[\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{kk}$ is large,
%% parameter $\phi_k$ cannot be reliably estimated \emph{regardless of the
%% estimation method employed}. No amount of algorithmic sophistication can
%% overcome a fundamental lack of information in the data.

We now derive the Fisher Information Matrix for our observation model
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:obs_gaussian}). Taking the partial derivative of the
log-likelihood (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglik}) with respect to a parameter
$\phi$:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t,i} \left[
\frac{1}{\mu_i(t)} - \frac{2\rho(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))}{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}
- \frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}\right]
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\end{equation*}

This can be written as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gradient}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi} = \sum_{t,i} A_i(t)
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*}
A_i(t) = -\frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)} + \frac{Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t)}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}
+ \frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}.
\end{equation*}
Note that this expression also gives us the gradient of the
log-likeihood function.


The Fisher Information Matrix is:
\begin{equation*}
J_{kl} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_l}\right] = \sum_{t,i}\sum_{s,j}
\mathbb{E}[A_i(t) A_j(s)] \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \mu_j(s)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{equation*}

We can easily verify that $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)]= 0$. Since observations
  are independent across time and regions, $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t) A_j(s)]=
  0$ when $(i,t) \neq (j,s)$. For the diagonal terms, we need
  $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)^2]$. Writing $\epsilon_i(t) = Y_i(t) -
  \rho\mu_i(t)$:
\begin{equation*}
A_i(t) = -\frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)} + \frac{\epsilon_i(t)}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}
+ \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^2}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
\end{equation*}

Expanding $A_i(t)^2$:
\begin{align*}
A_i(t)^2 &= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2} - \frac{\epsilon_i(t)}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
- \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^2}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^3} \\
&\quad + \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^2}{(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^3}{\rho(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^3}
+ \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^4}{4\rho^2(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^4}
\end{align*}

Using the moments of $\epsilon_i(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t))$:

\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon] &= 0, \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^2] &= \rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t), \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^3] &= 0, \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^4] &= 3[\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)]^2.
\end{align*}
  Taking expectations:
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)^2] &= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2}
- \frac{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^3}
+ \frac{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}{(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{3[\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)]^2}{4\rho^2(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^4} \\
&= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2} - \frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{\rho}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2} + \frac{3}{4\mu_i(t)^2} \\
&= \frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)^2} + \frac{\rho}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
= \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
\end{align*}

Therefore:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim_scalar}
J_{kl} = \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{\mu_i(t)^2} \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{equation}

Define the Jacobian $\mathbf{G} \in \mathbb{R}^{2T \times p}$ with
entries $G_{(i,t),k} = \partial \mu_i(t)/\partial \phi_k$, and note
that these are the incidence sensitivities from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:sens_mu}. Denote the diagonal weight matrix:
\begin{equation*}
\mathbf{W} = \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)} \,
\text{diag}\left(\mu_1(0)^{-2}, \ldots, \mu_n(T-1)^{-2}\right)
\end{equation*}
Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim}
\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{G}^T \mathbf{W} \mathbf{G}
\end{equation}
and we find the Cramer-Rao lower bound for the variance of $\theta$ as
$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq [\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{11}$.

\subsection{Observation Model}\label{subsec:noise}
We assume a constant reporting rate $\rho$, meaning a fraction $\rho$
of infections are observed. Each infection is reported independently
with probability $\rho$, so observations follow a binomial
distribution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs_binomial}
Y_i(t) \sim \text{Binomial}(\mu_i(t), \rho)
\end{equation}
with mean $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(t)] = \rho \mu_i(t)$ and variance
$\text{Var}[Y_i(t)] = \rho(1-\rho) \mu_i(t)$.

We approximate this distribution by a Gaussian via the central limit
theorem:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs_gaussian}
Y_i(t) = \rho \mu_i(t) + \epsilon_i(t), \quad
\epsilon_i(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_i^2(t))
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i^2(t) = \rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)$ is the observation
variance. This approximation is holds when $\mu_i(t)$ is large.
Under the Gaussian model, the log-likelihood of observing data
$\mathbf{Y} = \{Y_i(t)\}$ given parameters $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ is:
%% \begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
%% \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \prod_{t=0}^{T-1} \prod_{i=1}^{n}
%% \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_i^2(t)}}
%% \exp\left(-\frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{2\sigma_i^2(t)}\right)
%% \end{equation}
%% The log-likelihood is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
\ell(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
\left[\log(2\pi \sigma_i^2(t)) +
\frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{\sigma_i^2(t)}\right]
\end{equation}


\subsection{Maximum-likelihood estimates}\label{subsec:mle}
For given observed (fractional) incidence, we find the
maximum-likelihood estimate of $\theta$ by minimizing the negative
log-likelihood (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglik}) subject to $\theta \in [0,
  0.5]$, $S_i(0), I_i(0) \in [0,1]$, and $S_i(0) + I_i(0) \leq 1$. We
have already calculated the gradient of the log-likelihood in
eq.~\eqref{eq:score}. This gradient is computed exactly in a single
forward pass alongside the state and sensitivity equations.

We use the \texttt{NLopt} library with the SLSQP algorithm for
gradient-based optimization. To handle potential local minima, we
employ a multi-start strategy with $500$ random initial points,
selecting the solution with lowest objective value.

\subsection{Real Data}
To validate our theoretical predictions on empirical data, we analyze
pneumonia and influenza (P\&I) mortality in the United States. We
integrate two publicly available data sources: weekly P\&I death
counts from the CDC FluView surveillance system
\cite{cdcfluview2024} and the HealthData.gov P\&I mortality
dataset \cite{healthdatagov2024}. Where these sources overlap
temporally and geographically, we average the reported death counts,
as the two datasets show strong agreement (see Results). Population
denominators are obtained from the CDC WONDER database
\cite{cdcwonder2024} and interpolated linearly to weekly resolution.

The observation model for mortality data uses an infection fatality
rate (IFR) as the reporting rate $\rho$. Following
\citet{wong2013case}, we estimate the IFR for seasonal influenza as
approximately $\rho \approx 5.4 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.054\%), computed
from the ratio of reported P\&I deaths to estimated total infections.

The seasonal phase $\varphi_i$ for each region is estimated from
absolute humidity data \cite{shaman2010absolute}. We find the phase
offset that maximizes the correlation between $\sin(2\pi t +
\varphi)$ and negative absolute humidity, aligning the model's
seasonal forcing with humidity-driven transmission dynamics.

For both CRLB computation and parameter inference, we exclude
observations with incidence below $10^{-6}$ per capita. Such low
values provide negligible Fisher information and can numerically
destabilize the bound calculation through division by near-zero
incidence in the weight matrix.
