\section{Methods}

\subsection{Model}\label{subsec:model}

Consider two interconnected regions with populations $N_1$ and $N_2$.
Let $S_i(t)$, $I_i(t)$, $R_i(t)$ denote the number of susceptible,
infected, and recovered individuals in region $i$ at time $t$, with
$S_i + I_i + R_i = N_i$. The transmission rate has seasonal forcing:
$\beta_i(t) = \beta_0(1 + \delta\sin(2\pi t + \varphi_i))$, where
$t$ is measured in years. Regional coupling is governed by the contact
matrix
$\mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 1{-}\theta & \theta \\ \theta &
  1{-}\theta \end{bmatrix}$,
where $\theta \in [0, 0.5]$ is the connectivity parameter.

Following~\citet{edlund2011}, the force of
infection~\citep{keeling2008} in region $i$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:foi}
\lambda_i(t) = \beta_i(t) \frac{\sum_j C_{ij}\, I_j(t)}{\sum_j C_{ij}\, N_j}
\end{equation}
This normalizes by the contact-weighted population rather than by
each region's population independently, correctly accounting for the
distribution of contacts across regions.

The expected incidence is $\mu_i(t) = S_i(t)[1 -
\exp(-\lambda_i(t))]$ and the discrete-time SIR dynamics are:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:discrete_sir}
S_i(t{+}1) &= S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t)) \\
I_i(t{+}1) &= I_i(t) \exp(-\gamma) + \mu_i(t)
\end{split}\end{align}
We use SIR rather than SIRS dynamics because we conduct inference for
each influenza season separately. Model parameters are calibrated to
seasonal influenza following~\citet{shaman2010}; see
Table~\ref{tab:params}.

\subsection{Observation Model and Likelihood}\label{subsec:noise}

Each infection is reported independently with probability $\rho$,
approximated as Gaussian: $Y_i(t) = \rho\mu_i(t) + \epsilon_i(t)$
with $\epsilon_i(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \rho(1{-}\rho)\mu_i(t))$.
The unknown parameters for a single season are
$\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\theta, S_1(0), I_1(0), S_2(0), I_2(0))$,
giving the log-likelihood:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
\ell(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{2}
\left[\log(2\pi \sigma_i^2(t)) + \frac{(Y_i(t) -
    \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{\sigma_i^2(t)}\right]
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i^2(t) = \rho(1{-}\rho)\mu_i(t)$.

\subsection{Sensitivity Equations}\label{subsec:sens}

Computing the CRLB and optimizing the likelihood both require
derivatives of model outputs with respect to parameters. Let $\phi
\in \{\theta, S_1(0), S_2(0), I_1(0), I_2(0)\}$ denote a parameter
($\phi = \phi_k$ for $k = 1, \dots, 5$). Define
$\Omega := \partial \mathbf{C}/\partial \theta =
\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$
and note $\partial \mathbf{C}/\partial \phi = \mathbf{0}$ for $\phi
\neq \theta$.

For the force of infection~\eqref{eq:foi}, the sensitivity is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_lambda}
\frac{\partial \lambda_i}{\partial \phi} = \beta_i(t) \left[
\frac{1}{(\mathbf{C}\mathbf{N})_i}\left(
  \frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \phi} \mathbf{I}
  + \mathbf{C} \frac{\partial \mathbf{I}}{\partial \phi}\right)_i
- \frac{\lambda_i}{\beta_i (\mathbf{C}\mathbf{N})_i}
  \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \phi} \mathbf{N}\right)_i
\right]
\end{equation}
where the second term arises from differentiating the denominator
$(\mathbf{C}\mathbf{N})_i = \sum_j C_{ij} N_j$ with respect to
$\theta$. For $\phi \neq \theta$, $\partial \mathbf{C}/\partial \phi
= 0$ and only the $\mathbf{C}\,\partial\mathbf{I}/\partial\phi$ term
survives.

The sensitivity of the expected incidence follows by the product rule:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_mu}
\frac{\partial \mu_i}{\partial \phi} =
\frac{\partial S_i}{\partial \phi} [1 - e^{-\lambda_i}]
+ S_i e^{-\lambda_i} \frac{\partial \lambda_i}{\partial \phi}
\end{equation}

The state sensitivities propagate according to:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:sens}
\frac{\partial S_i(t{+}1)}{\partial \phi} &= e^{-\lambda_i}
\frac{\partial S_i}{\partial \phi} - S_i e^{-\lambda_i}
\frac{\partial \lambda_i}{\partial \phi} \\
\frac{\partial I_i(t{+}1)}{\partial \phi} &= e^{-\gamma}
\frac{\partial I_i}{\partial \phi} +
\frac{\partial \mu_i}{\partial \phi}
\end{split}\end{align}
with initial conditions $\partial S_i(0)/\partial S_j(0) =
\delta_{ij}$, $\partial I_i(0)/\partial I_j(0) = \delta_{ij}$, and
zero otherwise. These are solved jointly with the state equations in a
single forward pass.

\subsection{Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound}\label{subsec:crlb}

The CRLB states that $\text{Var}(\hat\phi_k) \geq
[\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{kk}$, where $\mathbf{J}$ is the $5 \times 5$
Fisher Information Matrix with entries $J_{kl} =
\mathbb{E}[(\partial\ell/\partial\phi_k)(\partial\ell/\partial\phi_l)]$.
Differentiating the log-likelihood~\eqref{eq:loglik} and defining
\begin{equation*}
A_i(t) := -\frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)} + \frac{Y_i(t) -
  \rho\mu_i(t)}{(1{-}\rho)\mu_i(t)} + \frac{(Y_i(t) -
  \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{2\rho(1{-}\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
\end{equation*}
yields $\partial \ell / \partial \phi = \sum_{t,i} A_i(t)\,\partial
\mu_i(t)/\partial \phi$. Since $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)] = 0$ and
observations are independent across $(i,t)$, we obtain (using
Gaussian moments of $\epsilon_i(t) = Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t)$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim_scalar}
J_{kl} = \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{2}
\frac{1}{\mu_i(t)^2} \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{equation}
In matrix form,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim}
\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{G}^T \mathbf{W} \mathbf{G}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{G} \in \mathbb{R}^{2T \times 5}$ has entries
$G_{(i,t),k} = \partial \mu_i(t)/\partial \phi_k$ and $\mathbf{W} =
\frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)}\,\text{diag}(\mu_1(0)^{-2}, \ldots,
\mu_2(T{-}1)^{-2})$.

When multiple seasons are available, we assume independence across
seasons and aggregate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crlb_multi}
\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \left(\sum_{s} \frac{1}{
  [\mathbf{J}^{-1}(s)]_{11}}\right)^{-1}
\end{equation}
treating each season's initial conditions as nuisance parameters.
Seasons where the CRLB computation fails (e.g., due to near-zero
incidence producing a singular Fisher matrix) contribute no
information and are assigned $[\mathbf{J}^{-1}(s)]_{11} = \infty$.

\subsection{Simulated CRLB Analysis}\label{subsec:sim_crlb}

To assess how synchronization and initial-condition similarity affect
identifiability, we compute the CRLB across a grid of connectivity
values $\theta \in [10^{-4}, 10^{-1}]$ and seasonal forcing
amplitudes $\delta \in [0, 1)$, comparing synchronized ($\varphi_1 =
\varphi_2$) and asynchronous ($\varphi_1 - \varphi_2 = \pi$)
epidemics. For each grid point, we draw random initial conditions
from one of two regimes: \emph{similar} ($S_i(0)/N_i \in [0.7,
0.95]$, $I_i(0)/N_i \in [10^{-5}, 10^{-3}]$, drawn independently
for both regions from the same range) and \emph{different} ($S_i(0)/N_i
\in [0.5, 0.95]$, $I_i(0)/N_i \in [10^{-5}, 10^{-2}]$). We repeat
each configuration many times with fresh random initial conditions and
report the average statistical power for a one-sided test of $\theta >
0$ at the 5\% significance level, assuming an efficient estimator that
achieves the CRLB. For the likelihood surface analysis, initial
conditions are drawn from the empirical distribution of fitted values
obtained during the real-data inversion (SI~Fig.~S3).

\subsection{Estimation}\label{subsec:mle}

We maximize the likelihood using gradient-based optimization (SLSQP
via NLopt) with analytical gradients from the sensitivity equations.
To handle potential local minima, we employ a multi-start strategy
with 200 random initial points, selecting the solution with lowest
objective value.

\subsection{Influenza Mortality Data}\label{subsec:real_data}

We analyze pneumonia and influenza (P\&I) mortality across 47 US
states (excluding Alaska, Hawaii, and Arizona), integrating CDC
FluView data (2010--2018)~\citep{cdcfluview2024} and HealthData.gov
data (2017--2025)~\citep{healthdatagov2025}, excluding pandemic years
2020--2022. Alaska and Hawaii are excluded because they lack absolute
humidity data for phase estimation; Arizona is excluded because its
arid climate produces humidity patterns inconsistent with the
sinusoidal forcing model. Since P\&I deaths include a year-round
non-influenza baseline, we estimate influenza burden as excess
mortality above a pre-season baseline, computed as the mean weekly
death count during July--October preceding each flu
season~\citep{serfling1963}. The infection fatality rate $\rho
\approx 5.36 \times 10^{-4}$ follows~\citet{rolfes2018}. Seasonal
phases $\varphi_i$ are estimated from absolute humidity
data~\citep{daon2025, shaman2009} by maximizing the correlation
between $-\text{AH}(t)$ and $\sin(2\pi t + \varphi)$. The median
phase difference between state pairs is approximately $\pi/45$,
reflecting the strong synchronization of temperate US influenza
seasons. We define each flu season as $T = 20$ weekly observations
starting November~1$^{\text{st}}$ and fit a single connectivity
$\theta$ shared across all seasons, with per-season initial
conditions $(S_i(0), I_i(0))$ as free parameters. We then aggregate
the per-season CRLBs via Eq.~\eqref{eq:crlb_multi} to obtain a bound
on the overall estimator variance.
