\section{Methods}
Our goal is to find a lower bound for estimation of a connectivity
parameter between regions. Our strategy is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Formulate a discrete SIR model for regional connectivity in
  terms of a connectivity parameter $\theta \in[0,0.5]$ (Section
  \ref{subsec:model}).
\item Calculate sensitivities for this model, i.e.~the derivatives of
  model output with respect to initial conditions and $\theta$
  (Section \ref{subsec:sens}).
\item Derive an observation model based on a constant reporting rate
  and approximate it as multiplicative Gaussian noise (Section
  \ref{subsec:noise}).
\item Find a maximum-likelihood estimate for $\theta$ and the initial
  conditions over several seasons (Section \ref{subsec:mle}).
\item Calculate a Cramer-Rao lower bound for the variance of the
  maximum-likelihood estimate of the connectivity parameter $\theta$
  (Section \ref{subsec:crlb}).
\end{enumerate}
We apply this approach to estimate connectivity $\theta$ between US
states and find accompanying a lower bound on the variance of the
estimator.


\subsection{Discrete-Time Connectivity Model}\label{subsec:model}
Consider $2$ interconnected regions with known population sizes $N_1$
and $N_2$ (not necessarily equal). Let $S_i(t)$, $I_i(t)$, and
$R_i(t)$ denote the number of Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered
individuals in region $i$ at time $t$, with $S_i(t) + I_i(t) + R_i(t)
= N_i$. We let $\beta_i(t)$ be a region-specific time-varying
transmission rate with seasonal forcing:
\begin{equation*}
\beta_i(t) = \beta_0\left(1 + \delta\sin\left(2\pi t + \varphi_i\right)\right).
\end{equation*}
Here, $\beta_0$ is the baseline transmission rate, $\delta$ is the
seasonal amplitude, and $\varphi_i$ is a phase offset for region
$i$. We assume time $t$ is measured in years.

We denote by $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$ a contact matrix
with entries $C_{ij} \in [0,1]$ that represents relative connectivity
from region $j$ to region $i$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact}
\mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 1-\theta & \theta \\ \theta & 1-\theta \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Here, $\theta \in [0, 0.5]$ is the connectivity parameter representing
the fraction of contacts that occur between regions. When $\theta =
0$, the regions are isolated; when $\theta = 0.5$, contacts are
equally distributed within and between regions. We enforce $\theta <
0.5$ since we do not expect more individuals to travel from region 1 to 2
than the number of individuals staying in 1.

The force of infection $\lambda_i(t)$~\citep{keeling2008} is the
per-capita rate at which susceptible individuals in region $i$ acquire
infection:
\begin{equation*}
\lambda_i(t) = \beta_i(t) \sum_{j=1}^{2} C_{ij} \frac{I_j(t)}{N_j}.
\end{equation*}
Treating $\lambda_i(t)$ as a constant hazard rate over one time step,
the probability that a susceptible individual \emph{escapes} infection
is $\exp(-\lambda_i(t))$, so the expected number of new infections in
region $i$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:incidence}
\mu_i(t) = S_i(t) \left[1 - \exp(-\lambda_i(t))\right]
\end{equation}

Similarly, the probability an infected individual remains infected
over one time step is $\exp(-\gamma)$, where $\gamma$ is the recovery
rate. The discrete-time SIR dynamics are thus:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:discrete_sir}
S_i(t+1) &= S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t)) \\
I_i(t+1) &= I_i(t) \exp(-\gamma) + \mu_i(t) \\
R_i(t+1) &= R_i(t) + I_i(t) [1 - \exp(-\gamma)]
\end{split}\end{align}

We model influenza via a SIR dynamics and not SIRS dynamics because
later we will conduct inference for every influenza season separately.

\subsection{Observation Model}\label{subsec:noise}
We assume a constant reporting rate $\rho$ so that each infection is
reported independently with probability $\rho$. Thus, observations
follow a binomial distribution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs_binomial}
Y_i(t) \sim \text{Binomial}(\mu_i(t), \rho)
\end{equation}
with mean $\mathbb{E}[Y_i(t)] = \rho \mu_i(t)$ and variance
$\text{Var}[Y_i(t)] = \rho(1-\rho) \mu_i(t)$.

We approximate this distribution by a Gaussian via the central limit
theorem:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs_gaussian}
Y_i(t) = \rho \mu_i(t) + \epsilon_i(t), \quad
\epsilon_i(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_i^2(t))
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i^2(t) = \rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)$ is the observation
variance. This approximation holds when $\rho\mu_i(t)$ is large.

We collect the unknown parameters pertaining to a single influenza
season in a vector:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:params}
  \boldsymbol{\phi} = \left( \theta, S_1(0), I_1(0), S_2(0),
  I_2(0)\right )
\end{equation}
The log-likelihood of observing data $\{Y_i(t)\}$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
\ell(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{2}
\left[\log(2\pi \sigma_i^2(t)) + \frac{(Y_i(t) -
    \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{\sigma_i^2(t)}\right]
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the length of the influenza season.


\subsection{Sensitivity equations}\label{subsec:sens}
Computing the Cram\'er-Rao lower bound and optimizing the likelihood
both require derivatives of model outputs with respect to
parameters. Let $\phi \in \{\theta, S_1(0), S_2(0), I_1(0), I_2(0)\}$,
(i.e.~$\phi=\phi_k$ for some $k=1,\dots,5$, e.g.~$\phi_3=I_1(0)$ from
eq.~\eqref{eq:params}) denote a parameter. Denote:
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{S}(t)&=\left (S_1(t), S_2(t)\right )\\
  \mathbf{I}(t)&=\left (I_1(t), I_2(t)\right )
\end{align*}
the vectors of Susceptibles and Infecteds; $\mathbf{C}$ still denotes
the connectivity matrix. Define $\mathbf{N}^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/N_1,
1/N_2)$ as the diagonal matrix of inverse population sizes.

The sensitivity of the force
of infection is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_lambda}
\frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi} = \beta_i(t) \left(
\frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \phi} \mathbf{N}^{-1} \mathbf{I}(t) + \mathbf{C} \mathbf{N}^{-1}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{I}(t)}{\partial \phi} \right)_i.
\end{equation}
Note that:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{C}}{\partial \theta} =
\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
while $\partial \mathbf{C}/\partial \phi = \mathbf{0}$ for $\phi \neq
\theta$.

The sensitivity of the expected incidence follows from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:incidence} by the product rule:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sens_mu}
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi} =
\frac{\partial S_i(t)}{\partial \phi} \left[1 - \exp(-\lambda_i(t))\right]
+ S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t)) \frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi}.
\end{equation}

The state sensitivities propagate, using eq.~\eqref{eq:discrete_sir},
according to:
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:sens}
\frac{\partial S_i(t+1)}{\partial \phi} &= \exp(-\lambda_i(t))
\frac{\partial S_i(t)}{\partial \phi} - S_i(t) \exp(-\lambda_i(t))
\frac{\partial \lambda_i(t)}{\partial \phi} \\
\frac{\partial I_i(t+1)}{\partial \phi} &= \exp(-\gamma)
\frac{\partial I_i(t)}{\partial \phi} +
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\end{split}\end{align}

Initial conditions for the sensitivities are: $\partial
S_i(0)/\partial S_j(0) = \delta_{ij}$, $\partial I_i(0)/\partial
I_j(0) = \delta_{ij}$, and zero otherwise. We solve the sensitivity
equations jointly with the state equations in a single forward pass.

\subsection{The Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound}\label{subsec:crlb}
Cram\'er and Rao derived a lower bound for the achievable variance of
an estimator, and here we focus on a bound for
$\text{Var}(\theta)$. The Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound, applied for a
single influenza season, states that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crlb_vector}
\text{Var}(\hat{\phi}_k) \geq [\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{kk}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{J}$ is the $5 \times 5$ \emph{Fisher Information
Matrix} with entries
\begin{equation*}
J_{kl} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_k}
  \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_l}\right].
\end{equation*}
We let $\phi \in \{\theta, S_1(0), S_2(0), I_1(0), I_2(0)\}$ and use
eq.~\eqref{eq:loglik}:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{t,i} \left[
\frac{1}{\mu_i(t)} - \frac{2\rho(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))}{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}
- \frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}\right]
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}
\end{equation*}
Denote
\begin{equation*}
A_i(t) := -\frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)} + \frac{Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t)}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}
+ \frac{(Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t))^2}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}.
\end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gradient}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi} = \sum_{t,i} A_i(t)
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi}.
\end{equation}
The Fisher Information Matrix is thus:
\begin{equation*}
J_{kl} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_l}\right] = \sum_{t,i}\sum_{s,j}
\mathbb{E}[A_i(t) A_j(s)] \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \mu_j(s)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{equation*}
We can easily verify that $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)]= 0$ and
$\mathbb{E}[A_i(t) A_j(s)]= 0$ for $(i,t) \neq (j,s)$, since
observations are independent across time and regions. Therefore
\begin{align}\begin{split}\label{eq:our fim}
J_{kl} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \phi_l}\right] = \sum_{t,i}
\mathbb{E}[A^2_i(t)] \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \mu_j(s)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{split}\end{align}
To find $\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)^2]$ we first expand $A_i(t)^2$:
\begin{align*}
A_i(t)^2 &= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2} - \frac{\epsilon_i(t)}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
- \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^2}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^3} \\
&\quad + \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^2}{(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^3}{\rho(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^3}
+ \frac{\epsilon_i(t)^4}{4\rho^2(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^4}
\end{align*}
where $\epsilon_i(t) = Y_i(t) - \rho\mu_i(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,
\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t))$. Using the moments of $\epsilon_i(t)$:
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon] &= 0, \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^2] &= \rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t), \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^3] &= 0, \\
  \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^4] &= 3[\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)]^2.
\end{align*}
Then
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}[A_i(t)^2] &= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2}
- \frac{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}{2\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^3}
+ \frac{\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)}{(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{3[\rho(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)]^2}{4\rho^2(1-\rho)^2\mu_i(t)^4} \\
&= \frac{1}{4\mu_i(t)^2} - \frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)^2}
+ \frac{\rho}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2} + \frac{3}{4\mu_i(t)^2} \\
&= \frac{1}{2\mu_i(t)^2} + \frac{\rho}{(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2} \\
&= \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)\mu_i(t)^2}
\end{align*}
and substituting in eq.~\eqref{eq:our fim}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim_scalar}
J_{kl} = \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{2}
\frac{1}{\mu_i(t)^2} \frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_k}
\frac{\partial \mu_i(t)}{\partial \phi_l}
\end{equation}
Define the Jacobian $\mathbf{G} \in \mathbb{R}^{2T \times 5}$ with
entries $G_{(i,t),k} = \partial \mu_i(t)/\partial \phi_k$and denote 
\begin{equation*}
\mathbf{W} = \frac{1+\rho}{2(1-\rho)} \,
\text{diag}\left(\mu_1(0)^{-2}, \ldots, \mu_2(T-1)^{-2}\right)
\end{equation*}
and then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fim}
\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{G}^T \mathbf{W} \mathbf{G}.
\end{equation}
We find the Cramer-Rao lower bound for the variance of $\theta$ for a
single season as $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq
[\mathbf{J}^{-1}]_{11}$.

When data from multiple influenza seasons are available, we exploit
the additivity of Fisher Information. We assume that seasons are
independent (each season has its own initial conditions but shares
$\theta$). Let $\mathbf{J}(s)$ denote the $5 \times 5$ Fisher
Information Matrix for season $s$. We treat the initial conditions as
nuisance parameters and find the Fisher Information for $\theta$ alone
as $1/ [\mathbf{J}^{-1}(s)]_{11}$. Since Fisher information is
additive for independent observations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crlb_multi}
\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \left(\sum_{s} \frac{1}{
  \left[\mathbf{J}^{-1}(s)\right]_{11}}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equation}


\subsection{Maximum-likelihood}\label{subsec:mle}
For given observed incidence over several seasons, we find
maximum-likelihood estimates of $\theta$ and initial conditions of
each season via eq.~\eqref{eq:loglik}. This optimization problem is
constrained: $\theta \in [0, 0.5]$, $S_i(0), I_i(0) \in [0, N_i]$, and
$S_i(0) + I_i(0) \leq N_i$. The gradient of the log-likelihood was
found in eq.~\eqref{eq:gradient}.

We use the \texttt{NLopt} library with the SLSQP algorithm for
gradient-based optimization. To handle potential local minima, we
employ a multi-start strategy selecting the solution with lowest
objective value. In experiments, results were identical for $20$ and
$200$ starting points but we take $200$ initial points for good
measure.

\subsection{Influenza mortality data}\label{subsec:real_data}
We analyze pneumonia and influenza (P\&I) mortality in the United
States. We integrate two publicly available data sources from the
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). Data for years
2010--2018 was obtained from the National Center for Health Statistics
(NCHS) Mortality Surveillance Data via the CDC FluView interactive
system \cite{cdcfluview2024}. More recent data (2017--2025) is sourced
from the Weekly Mortality Surveillance Data available on
HealthData.gov \cite{healthdatagov2024}. We discard data for the years
of the COVID-19 pandemic (2020--2022). Where the sources overlap
temporally (2017--2018) and geographically, we average the reported
death counts, as the two datasets are quite consistent. State
populations were obtained from the CDC WONDER database
\cite{cdcwonder2024} for July 1$^{\text{st}}$ of every year.

We follow common practice and estimate influenza burden as excess
mortality above a baseline mortality determined by averaging mortality
in months prior to each influenza season.

We take the infection fatality rate (IFR) as the
reporting rate $\rho$. Following \citet{rolfes2018}, we estimate the
IFR for seasonal influenza as $\rho \approx 5.36 \times 10^{-4}$
(0.0536\%), computed from the ratio of reported P\&I deaths to
estimated total infections.

The seasonal phase $\varphi_i$ for each state was estimated from
absolute humidity data which we have extracted before \cite{daon2025,
  shaman2009}. We find the phase offset that maximizes the correlation
between the negative of the absolute humidity and $\sin(2\pi t +
\varphi)$.

We split the data into seasons of $T=20$ weekly observations starting
each November 1$^{\text{st}}$ and find maximum likelihood estimates
for a single $\theta$ and per-season initial conditions,
simultaneously. We aggregate the CRLB across seasons using
eq.~\eqref{eq:crlb_multi}.
