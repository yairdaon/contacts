\section*{Introduction}
Inferring coupling strength between synchronized dynamical systems is a
fundamental challenge across the sciences. When oscillators are driven
by common forcing or coupled strongly enough to synchronize, their
dynamics become nearly indistinguishable---and the very coupling one
seeks to measure becomes invisible in the observations. This problem
arises in neural systems exhibiting coherent oscillations, ecological
communities driven by shared environmental cycles, and spatially
connected epidemics synchronized by seasonal forcing. Here we address
this challenge in the context of infectious disease epidemiology, where
abundant surveillance data and well-characterized dynamics provide an
ideal setting to develop and validate a theoretical framework for
assessing identifiability.

Inferring interactions from epidemic surveillance data is a central
challenge in infectious disease epidemiology. While the behavior of a
single infectious disease in an isolated population is fairly well
understood~\citep{keeling2008}, the behavior of multiple co-circulating
pathogens depends critically on their interactions~\citep{rohani2003,
  shrestha2013, domenech2019, kramer2024}. Childhood infections
exemplify the importance of such interactions: increased susceptibility
to pertussis following measles infection~\citep{coleman2015},
co-occurrence of scarlet fever and varicella~\citep{coleman2016},
immune suppression following measles causing increased mortality from
various infections~\citep{mina2015}, and competition for susceptible
individuals between measles and whooping cough~\citep{rohani2003}.
Similar complexity arises in mosquito-borne diseases, where
interactions between dengue, Zika, and chikungunya remain poorly
characterized despite extensive modeling efforts~\citep{reiner2013,
  estofolete2023}. Beyond pathogen-pathogen interactions, spatial
interactions governing disease spread between geographical regions are
equally fundamental to understanding disease ecology and designing
effective interventions.

Yet inferring interactions from surveillance data has proven remarkably
difficult. Convergent Cross Mapping (CCM), a method designed for causal
inference in nonlinear systems~\citep{sugihara2012}, was applied to
epidemiological settings to identify environmental drivers of
influenza~\citep{deyle2016} and climatic modulation of
dengue~\citep{rypdal2019}.
However,\cite{cobey2016} found the method ``extremely sensitive to
periodic fluctuations, inferring interactions between independent
strains that oscillate with similar frequencies.'' In response, the developers of CCM noted that such
results may reflect ``mistaking synchrony for
causation''~\citep{sugihara2017}---a fundamental identifiability
problem that we address directly in this study. Even likelihood-based
approaches face challenges: \citet{shrestha2011} found that while some
interaction parameters were ``extremely well identified,'' others
remained poorly constrained despite extensive data. These mixed results
highlight a recurring theme: shared seasonal forcing and synchronized
dynamics can create spurious signals or mask true interactions.

A similar challenge arises in spatial epidemiology, where researchers
attempt to infer connectivity between regions from case data. If
connectivity could be reliably estimated from surveillance data alone,
it would obviate the need for external mobility data sources. Several
studies have attempted such inference, with mixed results
\cite{kiss2024towards, kiss2023parameter}. A recurring finding is that transmission
rate and network connectivity are fundamentally confounded: ``the
product $p\beta$ can always be estimated but the individual factors
may be unidentifiable, making it difficult to distinguish between a
mildly transmissible disease on a highly connected network from a
highly transmissible infection on a sparse
network''\cite{welch2011}.

These identifiability challenges have led most spatial epidemic
studies to rely on external mobility data---from commuting surveys
\cite{viboud2006}, gravity models \cite{charu2017} or mobile phone
records~\cite{kraemer2020}---rather than attempting to infer
connectivity from case data. Yet such proxy measures may not
accurately reflect disease transmission pathways, and are not always
available~\cite{wu2013sensitivity}. The fundamental question remains:
under what conditions, if any, can connectivity be inferred directly
from epidemic surveillance data?

What determines whether interactions---be they between pathogens or
between regions---can be reliably inferred? Structural identifiability
analysis can determine whether parameters are theoretically
recoverable from ideal data~\cite{tuncer2018, chowell2023structural}
(!!!!!!!chowell was originally cited as villaverde WTF???). However,
structural identifiability is a binary classification that does not
quantify estimation precision. The \emph{Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound}
(CRLB) provides exactly this: a theoretical lower bound on the
variance of any unbiased estimator, derived from the Fisher
Information Matrix (find another ref for CRLB). If the CRLB for a
parameter is large, no estimation method---however sophisticated---can
reliably recover that parameter from the available data. Recent work
has shown that practical unidentifiability can severely hamper
epidemic predictions~\cite{gallo2022}. Computing the CRLB before
optimization can save substantial effort and prevent
overinterpretation of unreliable estimates.

Computing the CRLB requires the Jacobian matrix of model outputs with
respect to parameters, typically obtained via sensitivity equations.
For a system $\dot{x} = f(x, t, \beta)$, the sensitivities $s =
\partial x / \partial \beta$ satisfy $\dot{s} = (\partial f / \partial
x) s + \partial f / \partial \beta$, which can be solved jointly with
the original system~\cite{mester2022}. Previous implementations have
used numerical differentiation, perturbing each parameter and running
multiple simulations to approximate derivatives
\cite{eisenberg2014}. For continuous-time ODE models, software
packages such as \cite{mester2022} solve sensitivity equations
alongside the state equations using numerical integration. However,
these approaches face practical challenges: numerical differentiation
introduces approximation errors and requires $2p$ model evaluations
for $p$ parameters; continuous-time sensitivity analysis accumulates
integration errors over long time horizons and can suffer from
numerical instabilities in stiff systems.

In this study, we take a different approach. We formulate the epidemic
model in discrete time, matching the natural resolution of weekly
surveillance data. This discrete formulation yields \emph{exact,
  closed-form update equations} for both the state variables and their
sensitivities. Our approach offers several advantages:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Computational efficiency}: Sensitivities are computed in
  a single forward pass alongside the state, with no additional model
  evaluations required.

\item \textbf{Numerical stability}: Discrete updates avoid the
  accumulated integration errors of continuous-time solvers. There is
  no step-size selection, no stiffness to manage, and no solver
  tolerances to tune.

\item \textbf{Exact derivatives}: Unlike finite-difference
  approximations, our analytically-derived sensitivity equations
  introduce no approximation error. The Jacobian is computed to
  machine precision.

\item \textbf{Avoiding vanishing gradients}: In continuous systems,
  sensitivities can decay exponentially over long time horizons,
  particularly for stable systems. Our discrete formulation with
  bounded update coefficients (e.g., $e^{-\gamma} < 1$) maintains
  well-behaved sensitivity propagation.

\item \textbf{Natural alignment with data}: Surveillance data are
  reported in discrete intervals (daily, weekly). A discrete-time
  model matches this structure directly, avoiding the additional
  discretization step required when fitting continuous models to
  discrete observations.
\end{enumerate}

We apply this framework to a fundamental question in spatial
epidemiology: \emph{can regional connectivity be inferred from
  epidemic surveillance data?} Using a two-region SIR model with
seasonal forcing, we derive the complete sensitivity equations for the
connectivity parameter $\theta$ and compute the associated CRLB. We
then validate these theoretical predictions with multi-start
optimization experiments on synthetic data.

Our central finding is that \textbf{connectivity inference depends
  critically on epidemic synchrony}. When two regions experience
epidemics in phase---rising and falling together due to shared
seasonal forcing---the data contain almost no information about their
connectivity. Mathematically, the incidence patterns from connected
and unconnected populations become nearly indistinguishable, leading
to a large CRLB for $\theta$. In contrast, when epidemics are out of
phase (e.g., due to different seasonal timing or staggered
introductions), the differential epidemic dynamics reveal the
connectivity signal, and $\theta$ becomes identifiable.

This result provides a theoretical explanation for the difficulties
encountered in previous studies. \cite{cobey2016} observation that convergent cross-mapping
fails for ``strains that oscillate with similar frequencies'' reflects
the same phenomenon. Our CRLB analysis provides a unified theoretical
foundation: synchronized dynamics destroy the Fisher Information for
coupling parameters, making inference fundamentally impossible
regardless of the statistical method employed.

Our findings have immediate practical implications. Attempts to infer
connectivity from synchronized epidemics---such as influenza across
temperate regions with similar winter peaks---are likely to fail
regardless of the estimation method employed. This explains why
studies like \cite{viboud2006, kraemer2020} have relied on external
mobility data rather than inferring connectivity from case counts: for
synchronized seasonal epidemics, case data simply do not contain the
necessary information.  Conversely, connectivity inference may be
feasible when comparing regions with different epidemic timing, such
as Northern and Southern Hemisphere locations, or during the early
invasion phase of a pandemic when regional epidemics are naturally
asynchronous. More broadly, this ``look before you leap'' approach can
guide study design, temper expectations, and prevent the
misinterpretation of poorly identified parameter estimates as
meaningful epidemiological quantities.

The mathematical structure underlying our results---coupled oscillators
with shared periodic forcing---arises throughout the biological and
physical sciences. Neural populations receiving common input exhibit
synchronization that may similarly obscure their connectivity. Predator-prey
systems and metapopulations driven by shared seasonality face analogous
identifiability challenges when researchers attempt to infer dispersal
or interaction strengths from population time series. Our discrete-time
CRLB framework provides a computationally efficient tool for assessing
inference feasibility in any such system. While we focus here on
epidemiological applications, where the practical stakes are high and
validation data are abundant, we anticipate that the core
insight---that synchronization fundamentally limits identifiability of
coupling parameters---will prove broadly applicable across domains where
coupled oscillatory dynamics arise.
