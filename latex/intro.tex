\section{Introduction}
Operational influenza forecasting is a cornerstone of US public-health
preparedness. The CDC FluSight collaborative
challenge~\citep{reich2019} and its analogues for other
pathogens~\citep{johansson2019} produce probabilistic forecasts that
inform vaccine distribution, hospital staffing, and the timing of
public-health messaging each winter. Many of the participating models
are explicitly \emph{spatial}: they fit state- or region-level
incidence jointly and encode inter-regional connectivity as an input
derived from external mobility data such as airline passenger volumes,
commuting surveys, mobile-phone records, or gravity
models~\citep{viboud2006, charu2017, kraemer2020, zhang2026}. The
surveillance case data to which such models are fit are not themselves
used to learn connectivity---the standard practice is to import it,
not to infer it. That practice relies on two seemingly contradictory
assumptions: (a) spatial coupling matters for forecast quality, but
(b) spatial coupling cannot be inferred from surveillance data.

We ask a question on (b) and use that to arrive at a conclusion on
(a): For seasonal influenza in the United States, can surveillance
data inform us on inter-regional coupling? And if not - why? The point
of the question is not to suggest that forecasters should fit
connectivity from case data---they do not, and we do not recommend
that they start. The point is to ask whether the data have anything to
say about connectivity. If they do, then the imported coupling
structure can in principle be cross-checked or replaced by inference.
If they do not then the model's expected outputs are effectively
insensitive to the imported coupling value and curating, integrating,
and maintaining external mobility data for a seasonal-flu forecasting
model is not adding any predictive support. In that case, any
dependence on spatial coupling will not differ whether connectivity is
imported from mobility data or set to zero.

This failure mode is documented empirically. For example,
\citet{cobey2016} found that causal inference
methods~\citep{sugihara2012} are degraded when inferring interactions
between independent strains that oscillate with similar frequencies,
and \citet{sugihara2017} acknowledged that such findings may reflect
``mistaking synchrony for causation.''  Moreover, \citet{shrestha2011}
found that some pathogen-interaction parameters were identifiable
while others remained poorly constrained.  \citet{welch2011} showed
that transmission rate and network structure are fundamentally
confounded. Outside epidemiology, the same problem arises whenever
coupled oscillators are driven by common forcing~\citep{pikovsky2001}:
in neural circuits~\citep{reid2019, friston2011}, ecological
metapopulations~\citep{bjornstad1999, grenfell1998}, and climate
networks~\citep{tsonis2004architecture}.

These empirical findings share a common mechanism that is often
obscured by imprecise terminology. Three phenomena are routinely
conflated~\citep{pikovsky2001}: \emph{Synchronization} is the
phase-locking of two or more oscillators through mutual coupling;
\emph{resonance} is the amplified response of an oscillator to
periodic forcing near its natural frequency; and \emph{entrainment} is
what arises when several independent oscillators are each driven by
the same periodic forcing. Entrainment can be indistinguishable from
mutual synchronization by eye, but it requires no coupling between the
oscillators at all. Influenza in the United States fits in the
entrainment regime.

We employ the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound (CRLB)
\citep{casella2024statistical, wilke2020} for quantifying how
entrainment confounds connectivity. The CRLB gives a lower bound on
the variance of \emph{every} unbiased estimator of a model
parameter. The CRLB is practical for modeling for two reasons. First,
the CRLB applies to every estimator. Hence, the CRLB can inform a
policy maker what is possible and what is not, without the
trial-and-error related to swapping optimizers and likelihood
approximations. If the CRLB is large, no method can deliver reliable
inference and iterating through inference pipelines is wasted
effort. Second, because the CRLB is computed from the model and a
hypothesized parameter value rather than from fitted data, it can be
evaluated at the design stage. Together these properties make the CRLB
a pre-commitment feasibility test: the modeler should ask whether a
proposed inference can really contain information before any
engineering effort is sunk in. This is strictly stronger than
structural identifiability analysis, which answers a yes/no question
for the model in isolation but cannot quantify how much information
real, finite, noisy data actually carry about a given parameter;
structural-identifiability tools have been developed extensively in
systems biology~\citep{villaverde2014} and in
epidemiology~\citep{tuncer2018, chowell2023structural}. Overlooking
such practical-identifiability issues has been shown to severely
degrade epidemic predictions~\citep{gallo2022}.

We apply this framework to the question of inter-regional connectivity
for seasonal influenza. Using a discrete-time two-region SIR model
with seasonal forcing, we compute the CRLB for a connectivity
parameter. We then fit the model to 12 seasons of pneumonia and
influenza (P\&I) excess mortality across 47~US states.

Our finding is that \textbf{there is no statistical evidence for
  inter-regional coupling in US influenza mortality}. CRLB-derived
95\% confidence intervals around the fitted connectivity overlap zero
for nearly all 1081 state pairs. Connectivity estimates spread almost
uniformly across $[0, 0.5]$. The CRLB analysis explains why: each
state's epidemic is entrained to its local seasonal forcing, and the
resulting near-symmetry in the expected trajectories makes the data
fundamentally uninformative about connectivity. Therefore, our
practical recommendation for preparedness forecasting is therefore to
\textbf{drop inter-regional coupling from the model}.
