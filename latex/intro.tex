\section*{Introduction}
Spatial spread of infectious diseases depends critically on
connectivity patterns between geographical regions. Mathematical
models of epidemic dynamics typically incorporate contact matrices
that quantify transmission pathways between locations. %% These
%% connectivity parameters are fundamental to understanding disease
%% spread, designing intervention strategies, and predicting outbreak
%% trajectories across spatial networks.

I want to understand why we cannot infer connectivity from long-time
series of weekly incidence of (e.g.) influenza.

%% Traditional approaches to parameterizing contact matrices rely on
%% proxy data sources: airline passenger flows
%% \citep{hufnagel2004forecast}, commuting patterns
%% \citep{viboud2006synchrony}, or gravity models based on population and
%% distance \citep{xia2004measles}. However, these proxies may not
%% accurately reflect disease transmission pathways, particularly for
%% respiratory pathogens where transmission mechanisms differ from
%% mobility patterns.

%% This raises a fundamental question: can connectivity parameters be
%% inferred directly from epidemic surveillance data? Such an approach
%% would be attractive as it captures actual disease transmission
%% patterns rather than relying on potentially misaligned proxy
%% measures. However, the mathematical feasibility of this inverse
%% problem remains unclear.

%% In this paper, we demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that
%% connectivity inference from epidemic data is fundamentally
%% intractable. Using multi-region SEIR models, we show that seasonal
%% forcing drives epidemic synchronization across regions, rendering the
%% infection matrix nearly rank-deficient. This leads to catastrophic
%% error amplification, where small measurement errors translate to
%% arbitrarily large errors in connectivity estimates.

%% We validate these theoretical predictions using Vector Autoregression
%% analysis of 30 years of pneumonia and influenza data across US
%% states. Models incorporating inter-state connectivity show no
%% significant improvement over independent state-level models,
%% confirming that connectivity information cannot be reliably extracted
%% from epidemic time series.
