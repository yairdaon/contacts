\section{Introduction}
Operational influenza forecasting is a cornerstone of US public-health
preparedness. The CDC FluSight collaborative challenge
\citep{reich2019} and its analogues for other pathogens
\citep{johansson2019} produce probabilistic forecasts that inform
vaccine distribution, hospital staffing, and other policy
decisions. Spatial analyses of US influenza and related respiratory
viruses have repeatedly shown that inter-regional spread is well
described by mobility data---commuting flows from US Census workflow
data~\citep{viboud2006, charu2017}, domestic airline passenger
volumes~\citep{charu2017, zhang2026}, and mobile-phone movement
indices in emergent-outbreak settings~\citep{kraemer2020}---and
spatial epidemic models therefore routinely encode inter-regional
connectivity as an input derived from these external sources rather
than from case data themselves. A natural modelling question sits
beneath this practice: does information from other regions actually
help forecast a given region's flu incidence, and if so, what is the
source of the improvement?

%\subsection{Evidence for employing spatial models}
Several flu-forecasting systems exploit cross-state structure
explicitly \citep{pei2018, osthus2021, lu2019, aiken2021, deng2020}.
%% state-level metapopulation SIRS models with commuting-flow
%% connectivity between states~\citep{pei2018}, multi-scale Bayesian
%% joint forecasters that share information across state, regional and
%% national scales~\citep{osthus2021}, network-based nowcasters that
%% exploit state-to-state ILI synchronicities~\citep{lu2019},
%% multi-resolution recurrent neural networks~\citep{aiken2021}, and
%% graph-attention networks~\citep{deng2020}.
Whether this added complexity translates into better forecasts is,
however, contested.  For example, \citet{kramer2020} found that a
12-country European mechanistic metapopulation linked by commuting and
air-travel flows did \emph{not} outperform isolated single-country
forecasts. On the other hand, standard time-series tools suggest that
ILI forecasting for US states benefits from including neighbour-state
as covariates in various autoregression methods \citet{thivierge2025},
where a modest but consistent improvement in forecast skill was
found. This qualitative picture is reproduced in
Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} (see Methods for details). However, the
results presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} are somewhat
misleading, as we will later observe.

The forecasting-level evidence therefore seems to justify the standard
practice of including an inter-regional coupling component in spatial
influenza models. The question we address in this paper is whether
that justification is sound.

%\subsection{Entrainment, resonance, and synchronization.}
One major characteristic of seasonal influenza epidemics is
\emph{entrainment} \citep{pikovsky2001}.
%% : \emph{synchronization} is the phase-locking of two or more
%% oscillators through mutual coupling; \emph{resonance} is the amplified
%% response of an oscillator to periodic forcing near its natural
%% frequency; and
Entrainment occurs when an independent oscillator is driven by a
periodic forcing. Entrainment can be indistinguishable from mutual
synchronization, but unlike synchronization it requires only a
unidirectional coupling. In the United States, all states share a
similar intrinsic transmission dynamics and are driven by similar
annual environmental forcing. Thus, state-level mortality trajectories
can be strongly correlated without any inter-state coupling.  The
distinction between synchronization and entrainment leads us to ask
\textbf{are seasonal influenza epidemics synchronized or only
  entrained?} An answer to this question has immediate implications
for influenza forecasting.
%% The empirical signature of this mis-identification is
%% well-documented. \citet{cobey2016} found that causal-inference methods
%% applied to oscillating time series \citep{sugihara2012} are degraded
%% when the series share frequencies, and \citet{sugihara2017}
%% acknowledged that such findings may reflect ``mistaking synchrony for
%% causation.''
%% \citet{shrestha2011} found that some pathogen-interaction
%% parameters were identifiable while others were poorly constrained;
%% Indeed, \citet{welch2011} showed that transmission rate and network
%% structure are fundamentally confounded.
Outside epidemiology the same problem arises whenever coupled
oscillators are driven by common forcing \citep{pikovsky2001}: in
neural circuits \citep{reid2019, friston2011}, ecological
metapopulations \citep{bjornstad1999, grenfell1998}, and climate
networks \citep{tsonis2004architecture}.

%% \subsection{The Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound as a formal diagnostic.}
In order to distinguish between entrainment and synchronization, we
employ the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound (CRLB henceforth)
\citep{casella2024statistical, wilke2020}. The CRLB gives a lower
bound on the variance of \emph{every} unbiased estimator of a model
parameter, and can therefore rule out the existence of any usable
inference method for that parameter. This is stronger than structural
identifiability analysis \citep{villaverde2014, tuncer2018,
  chowell2023structural}, which answers a yes/no question for the
model in isolation; the CRLB quantifies how much information real,
finite, noisy data can plausibly carry.

%% \paragraph{Outline of findings.}

Applying the CRLB to a discrete-time two-region SIR model with
seasonal forcing, we show that the connectivity parameter $\theta$ is
unidentifiable under entrainment. We then employ the CRLB to derive
CIs around maximum-likelihood estimates of $\theta$ using 12 seasons
of P\&I excess mortality across 47~states. For nearly all state pairs,
we cannot distinguish $\theta > 0$ from $\theta=0$. This implies that
the forecast improvement in Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} is not due
to a connectivity parameter. We then replace the covariate in the
SARIMA forecast by a national average of per-capita weekly mortality
and obtain a considerable improvement in forecast skill. This result
demonstrates that the results in Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} are
almost entirely due to shared national-level seasonal severity rather
than inter-state interactions.

Our practical recommendation is direct. For endemic seasonal pathogens
with similar drivers, connectivity is not a necessary component for
forecasting models. Forecasts that aggregate national-level
information into ``seasonal severity'' capture essentially all of the
cross-state signal available in the surveillance data.
