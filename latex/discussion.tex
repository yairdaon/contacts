\section*{Discussion}

We have shown that phase synchronization between coupled epidemic
systems fundamentally limits the identifiability of connectivity
parameters. Using the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound, we quantified this
limitation precisely: when epidemics rise and fall together, the
Fisher Information for coupling parameters approaches zero, making
reliable inference impossible regardless of the statistical method
employed. Multi-start optimization on synthetic data confirmed these
theoretical predictions.

\subsection*{Implications for Global Influenza Surveillance}

Our results have direct implications for inferring influenza
transmission between geographical regions.
\href{https://www.pnas.org/content/113/46/13081}{Deyle et al.\ (2016)}
demonstrated that influenza dynamics differ fundamentally between
temperate and tropical regions: in temperate countries like Germany,
the seasonal climate cycle explains over 90\% of the variance in
absolute humidity, driving tightly synchronized winter epidemics. In
tropical countries like Singapore, seasonality explains less than 30\%
of climate variance, resulting in irregular, weakly synchronized
outbreaks.

This dichotomy maps directly onto our identifiability framework.
\textbf{In temperate regions}---North America, Europe, northern
Asia---influenza epidemics are strongly synchronized by shared winter
forcing. Our analysis predicts that connectivity between such regions
cannot be reliably inferred from case data alone, explaining why
studies of influenza spread in these regions have relied on external
mobility
data~\href{https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1125237}{[Viboud
    et al., 2006]},
\href{https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005382}{[Charu
    et al., 2017]}. \textbf{In tropical regions}---sub-Saharan Africa,
South America, Southeast Asia, Polynesia---the weaker and less
coherent seasonal forcing produces asynchronous epidemic patterns.
Here, our framework predicts that connectivity inference may be
feasible, as the differential timing of outbreaks provides the
information needed to distinguish coupled from uncoupled dynamics.

This suggests a strategic reorientation of inference efforts: rather
than attempting to infer connectivity from synchronized temperate
epidemics, researchers should focus on tropical and subtropical
regions where asynchrony provides identifiability, or on pandemic
emergence phases when regional epidemics are naturally out of phase.

\subsection*{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant discussion. First, our analysis assumes a
specific observation model (binomial reporting with Gaussian
approximation); other noise structures may yield different CRLB
expressions. Second, we focused on a two-region model with symmetric
coupling; extensions to asymmetric coupling or larger networks may
reveal additional identifiability challenges. Third, our synthetic
data validation used the same model for generation and inference;
model misspecification in real applications could introduce additional
biases.

\subsection*{Broader Implications}

The mathematical structure underlying our results---coupled
oscillators with shared periodic forcing---extends beyond
epidemiology. Neural circuits receiving common input exhibit
synchronization that may similarly obscure functional connectivity.
Metapopulations driven by shared environmental cycles face analogous
challenges when researchers attempt to infer dispersal rates from
abundance time series. In each case, the core insight applies:
synchronization induced by common forcing destroys the information
needed to identify coupling, creating a fundamental confound between
``connected but synchronized'' and ``unconnected but similarly
driven.''

Our discrete-time CRLB framework provides a computationally efficient
tool for assessing inference feasibility before committing to costly
estimation procedures. We advocate a ``look before you leap''
approach: compute the CRLB for parameters of interest, and only
proceed with inference when the bound indicates that meaningful
precision is achievable.
