\section*{Discussion}

We have shown that the identifiability of connectivity between coupled
epidemic systems depends on the degree of symmetry between their
dynamics. Using the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound, we quantified this
precisely: when two regions experience synchronized epidemics with
similar initial conditions, the Fisher Information for the coupling
parameter approaches zero. A maximum-likelihood optimizer will still
converge in this regime, potentially returning an estimate that
appears well-determined but is in fact meaningless---the likelihood
surface is flat, and any value of $\theta$ fits the data equally well.
Any source of asymmetry---different seasonal phasing or different
initial epidemic states---breaks this degeneracy and restores
identifiability.

\subsection*{The Role of Symmetry Breaking}

Our analysis identifies two independent mechanisms that restore
identifiability. First, when the seasonal forcing phases differ
between regions, the epidemics peak at different times, and the
connectivity parameter governs the cross-regional transmission that
links these offset dynamics. Second, when initial conditions
differ---for example, because regions enter the flu season with
different levels of prior immunity---the resulting epidemic
trajectories diverge even under identical forcing, again creating a
signal that depends on $\theta$.

For US states, both mechanisms are weak. Seasonal influenza is driven
by winter conditions that are approximately synchronized across
temperate North America~\citep{shaman2010}, and population-level
susceptibility follows similar patterns nationwide. This double
symmetry explains why connectivity cannot be reliably inferred from
mortality data despite over a decade of observations.

\subsection*{Implications for Influenza Surveillance}

The contrast between temperate and tropical influenza dynamics maps
directly onto our identifiability framework. In temperate regions,
shared winter forcing drives tightly synchronized epidemics, placing
inference in the high-CRLB regime. In tropical regions, weaker and
less coherent seasonal forcing produces asynchronous outbreak
patterns~\citep{tamerius2013}, providing the asymmetry needed for
identifiability. This suggests that connectivity inference from case
data may be more feasible in tropical and subtropical settings, or
during the early invasion phase of a pandemic when regional epidemics
are naturally out of phase.

Our results also explain why spatial epidemic studies have
predominantly relied on external mobility data---commuting
surveys~\citep{viboud2006}, gravity models~\citep{charu2017},
mobile phone records~\citep{kraemer2020}, or air travel
data~\citep{zhang2026}---rather than estimating connectivity from
case counts. This was not necessarily a deliberate
choice informed by identifiability theory; rather, case-data-based
estimates were likely tried, found to be noisy or inconsistent, and
quietly abandoned in favor of external data sources. Our CRLB
analysis provides a theoretical explanation for these practical
failures.

\subsection*{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant discussion. First, our model assumes a
single influenza strain with uniform transmission parameters
($\beta_0$, $\delta$, $\gamma$) across all seasons. In reality, the
basic reproduction number varies substantially across influenza
subtypes: H3N2 seasons tend to be more severe and transmissible than
H1N1 or influenza B seasons~\citep{biggerstaff2014}. This
variability is partially mitigated by allowing the initial susceptible
fraction $S_i(0)$ to vary freely across seasons---a lower effective
$R_0$ can be compensated by a higher initial susceptible pool, and
vice versa---but the seasonal forcing shape is fixed.  A model that
explicitly accounts for strain-specific transmission could improve
real-data fits.

Second, we focused on a two-region model with symmetric coupling;
extensions to asymmetric coupling or larger networks may reveal
additional identifiability challenges. Third, our observation model
uses a Gaussian approximation to the binomial, which may be
inadequate for small states where weekly excess death counts are low.
Fourth, we compute excess deaths by subtracting a simple pre-season
baseline following the approach of~\citet{serfling1963}; more
sophisticated baseline models could improve the influenza-specific
signal.

\subsection*{Broader Implications}

Our results offer a partial explanation for the well-documented
difficulty of epidemic
forecasting~\citep{wilke2020, rosenkrantz2022}. When regional
epidemics are synchronized, the data cannot distinguish variability
due to seasonal forcing from variability due to importation from
connected regions. A model that attributes a surge to strong local
forcing with weak coupling fits the data just as well as one that
attributes the same surge to moderate forcing with strong coupling.
This unresolved confound propagates into forecasts: any prediction
that depends on the connectivity structure inherits the full
uncertainty of the unidentified coupling parameter. The problem is
not merely that connectivity is unknown---it is that the data are
fundamentally silent about it when the system is synchronized.

The mathematical structure underlying our results---coupled
oscillators with shared periodic forcing---extends beyond
epidemiology. Neural circuits receiving common input exhibit
synchronization that may similarly obscure functional
connectivity~\citep{reid2019, pikovsky2001}. Metapopulations driven by shared
environmental cycles face analogous challenges when researchers
attempt to infer dispersal rates from abundance time
series~\citep{bjornstad1999spatial}. In each case, the core insight
applies: \emph{it is not synchronization alone that destroys
  identifiability, but the combination of synchronization with
  symmetric initial states}. Any observable asymmetry---different
initial conditions, different intrinsic frequencies, or different
responses to forcing---provides leverage for distinguishing coupled
from uncoupled dynamics.

The practical implication is cautionary. When fitting models of
coupled systems to synchronized data, one should not trust the point
estimate alone. The CRLB provides a cheap diagnostic: if the
theoretical lower bound on estimator variance is large relative to
the estimate, then the estimate is unreliable no matter how good
the fit appears. We advocate computing the CRLB before investing
effort in optimization, and reporting it alongside any connectivity
estimate as a measure of the information content of the data.
