\begin{abstract}
Inferring connectivity between coupled dynamical systems is a
fundamental challenge when shared forcing synchronizes their dynamics.
We address this problem in the context of spatial epidemiology, asking:
can regional connectivity be inferred from epidemic surveillance data?
Using a discrete-time two-region SIR model with seasonal forcing, we
derive exact sensitivity equations and compute the Cram\'er-Rao Lower
Bound (CRLB) for the connectivity parameter. Our analysis reveals that
identifiability depends critically on epidemic synchrony. When regions
experience epidemics in phase---rising and falling together due to
shared seasonal forcing---the Fisher Information for connectivity
approaches zero, making reliable inference impossible regardless of
estimation method. In contrast, when epidemics are out of phase, the
differential dynamics reveal the connectivity signal. We validate these
theoretical predictions with multi-start optimization on synthetic data:
the likelihood surface is flat for synchronized epidemics but shows a
clear peak at the true connectivity for asynchronous epidemics. Our
results explain why previous studies have struggled to infer
connectivity from case data alone, and suggest that inference efforts
should focus on settings where regional epidemics are naturally
asynchronous. The discrete-time CRLB framework provides a
computationally efficient tool for assessing parameter identifiability
before attempting costly estimation.
\end{abstract}
