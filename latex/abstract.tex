\begin{abstract}
Contact matrices between geographical regions are fundamental to
epidemiological modeling, yet they are typically inferred from
external data sources such as flight patterns or commuting data. This
paper investigates whether connectivity between regions can be
reliably inferred directly from epidemic surveillance data. Using a
multi-region SEIR model with seasonal forcing, we demonstrate that the
inverse problem of contact matrix inference is fundamentally
ill-conditioned due to epidemic synchronization. Our theoretical
analysis shows that relative errors in connectivity estimates scale
with the condition number of the infection matrix, which grows
exponentially with the number of regions ($\kappa(M) \approx 10^5$ for
2 regions, $10^{11}$ for 10 regions). We empirically validate these
findings using 30 years of pneumonia and influenza surveillance data
across US states through Vector Autoregression (VAR) analysis. Models
incorporating inter-state connectivity show no significant improvement
in forecasting performance compared to independent state-level models,
confirming the theoretical predictions. Our results demonstrate that
epidemic synchrony, particularly under seasonal forcing, renders
connectivity inference intractable regardless of data quality or
analysis method.
\end{abstract}
