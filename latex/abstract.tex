\begin{abstract}
Spatial epidemic models for seasonal-influenza forecasting routinely
encode inter-regional connectivity, and standard time-series methods
suggest that forecasts are improved by using one state's lagged
incidence as a covariate for another's: across all 2162 ordered
directed pairs among 47 US states, a univariate seasonal-ARIMA model
augmented with the other state's lag-1 weekly mortality reduces
out-of-sample RMSE in 64\% of pairs (median 0.7\%). This apparent
gain motivates a prior question: does it reflect directed
inter-regional coupling, or a different phenomenon? Using the
Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound of a discrete-time two-region SIR model we
show that the connectivity parameter $\theta$ is fundamentally
unidentifiable under seasonal entrainment; a validation on 12 seasons
of excess pneumonia-and-influenza mortality across 47~US states
confirms the prediction, with CRLB-derived 95\% confidence intervals
around $\hat\theta$ overlapping zero for nearly all 1081 unordered
pairs. Replacing the bilateral covariate by a leave-one-out
national-average of per-capita weekly mortality yields an
order-of-magnitude larger forecast improvement (median 7.5\%, 97\% of
states helped), showing that the apparent cross-state signal is
shared national-level seasonal severity rather than directed
connectivity. For endemic seasonal pathogens such as influenza,
inter-regional coupling is not a necessary component of preparedness
forecasting models.
\end{abstract}
