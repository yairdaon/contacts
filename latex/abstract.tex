\begin{abstract}
Spatial epidemic models for seasonal-influenza forecasting routinely
encode inter-regional connectivity, and standard time-series methods
suggest that forecasts are improved by using one state's lagged
incidence as a covariate for another's. It is not clear, however,
whether this improvement reflects directed inter-regional coupling, or
perhaps a different phenomenon. To answer this question, we pursue two
parallel lines of analysis. First, we apply the Cram\'er-Rao Lower
Bound to a two-region SIR model and show that the connectivity
parameter is unidentifiable under seasonal entrainment. We validate
these results on 12 seasons of excess pneumonia and influenza
mortality across 47 US states and observe that 95\% confidence
intervals derived from the Cram\'er-Rao Lower Bound around the
estimated connectivity overlap zero for nearly all state
pairs. Second, we further validate our first analysis by investigating
forecasts of pneumonia and influenza mortality using a standard SARIMA
model. We employ two covariates: a second state's mortality, and the
national-average mortality. We show that the other state's mortality
provides no improvement in forecast skill beyond the national
average. These two lines of analysis converge on the same conclusion:
connectivity cannot be inferred from the data, and does not improve
forecasts beyond the shared national-level signal. Hence, connectivity
should be dropped from forecast models used for influenza preparedness
in accordance with Occam's razor.
\end{abstract}
