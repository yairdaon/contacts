\section{Results}

\subsection{Inter Regional Connectivity in the US via Influenza}
We analyze excess pneumonia and influenza mortality from 47 US states
over 12 flu seasons.  Figure~\ref{fig:seasons} shows the raw weekly
death counts for four representative states, with the flu seasons used
for inference highlighted.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/seasons_highlighted.pdf}
\caption{Weekly pneumonia and influenza deaths for four US
  states. Light traces show the full time series; darker segments
  indicate the 20-week flu seasons (starting each November) included
  in the analysis. COVID-19 pandemic years (2020--2022) are
  excluded. A pre-season baseline is subtracted before model fitting.}
\label{fig:seasons}
\end{figure}

We estimated $\theta$ and its CRLB for all $\binom{47}{2} = 1081$
state pairs across 12~seasons. Figure~\ref{fig:theta_vs_crlb} shows
$\hat\theta$ against $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$---the lower bound on the
standard deviation of any unbiased estimator of $\theta$, which the
MLE attains asymptotically. The dashed line marks the 5\% one-sided
significance boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\%
confidence intervals that overlap zero. The majority of state pairs
are above the line, so that for the vast majority of paris of states
the surveillance data provide no statistical evidence for $\theta >
0$. The estimates $\hat\theta$ themselves spread roughly uniformly
over $[0, 0.5]$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{../pix/theta_vs_crlb.pdf}
\caption{Estimated connectivity $\hat\theta$ vs.\ $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$
  for all 1081~US state pairs. The dashed line is the 5\% one-sided
  significance boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\%
  confidence intervals that overlap zero. Black circles:
  non-significant. Red stars: significant.}
\label{fig:theta_vs_crlb}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Entrainment Inflates the CRLB}
Figure~\ref{fig:crlb_ratio} shows the ratio of median CRLB
(synchronized / asynchronous) across a grid of connectivity values
$\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitudes $\delta$, with initial
conditions drawn from the empirical distribution of real-data fits
(SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). Across the $10\,000$ grid cells, the
median ratio is $17.3$ and the ratio exceeds unity in $97.9\%$ of
cells, with sub-unity values confined to the weakly-forced corner
($\delta \lesssim 0.01$) and never falling below $0.78$. The
95th-percentile ratio is $\sim 850$ and the maximum exceeds $10^{5}$.
Entrainment therefore inflates the minimum achievable estimation
variance of the connectivity parameter by a typical factor of order
ten and by factors that can exceed $10^{3}$ throughout most of the
parameter space, confirming, in a simulated setting with realistic
ICs, that entrainment dominates synchronization in the real data.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/crlb_ratio.pdf}
\caption{Ratio of median CRLB (synchronized / asynchronous) as a
  function of connectivity $\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitude
  $\delta$. Initial conditions for each simulated season and region
  are drawn independently from the empirical distribution of
  per-season, per-state values fitted to the real data
  (SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). Median ratio across the
  $100 \times 100$ grid is $17.3$; the ratio exceeds~1 in $97.9\%$ of
  cells.}
\label{fig:crlb_ratio}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Does a neighbour state add information beyond the national average?}\label{subsec:natavg_results}
We compared the four SARIMA configurations of
\S\ref{subsec:sarimabench} on the $1330$ ordered (target, neighbour)
pairs for which all four models converged. Including the lag-one
national average $\bar y_{t-1}$ alone reduces test-period RMSE
relative to the no-covariate baseline in $97\%$ of pairs, with a
median reduction of $7.4\%$. The single-state covariate $y_{B,t-1}$
alone reduces RMSE in only $65\%$ of pairs, with a median reduction of
$0.83\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap}).

The headline test asks whether the neighbour state adds any
information \emph{beyond} the national average---that is, whether the
joint covariate $(y_{B,t-1}, \bar y_{t-1})$ outperforms $\bar
y_{t-1}$ alone. It does not. Across the $1330$ pairs, the relative
RMSE change from adding $y_{B,t-1}$ on top of $\bar y_{t-1}$ has a
median of $+0.02\%$ and is negative in only $44\%$ of pairs
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_density}); the per-pair effect is
indistinguishable from a coin flip. Once the national-average
covariate is in the model, the neighbour state contributes nothing on
average.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/sarima_density.pdf}
\caption{Distribution of relative test-period RMSE change
  (eq.~\eqref{eq:sarima_delta}) when the neighbour state's lag-one
  mortality $y_{B,t-1}$ is added as a second exogenous covariate to a
  SARIMA that already includes the lag-one national average $\bar
  y_{t-1}$, across $1330$ ordered state pairs. Negative values mean
  adding the neighbour state improves the forecast. The distribution
  is nearly symmetric around zero (median $+0.02\%$, dashed red line),
  showing that the neighbour state carries no information beyond the
  national average.}
\label{fig:sarima_density}
\end{figure}

This is exactly the prediction of mechanism~\emph{(II)} of
\S\ref{subsec:sarimabench}: the modest bilateral effect visible in
Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} is not a signature of directed
inter-regional predictability, but a noisy proxy for shared
national-level severity that the national average captures cleanly.
Under genuine directed predictability, $y_{B,t-1}$ would carry
information not contained in $\bar y_{t-1}$ and the joint model would
beat the national-average-only model. We see no such effect.
