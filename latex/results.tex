\section{Results}

\subsection{Inter Regional Connectivity in the US via Influenza}

We analyze excess P\&I mortality from 47~US states over 12 flu seasons
(2010--2018 and 2023--2025, excluding the COVID-19 pandemic years).
Figure~\ref{fig:seasons} shows the raw weekly death counts for four
representative states, with the flu seasons used for inference
highlighted. The synchronized winter peaks are clearly visible: all
four time series rise and fall together each year.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/seasons_highlighted.pdf}
\caption{Weekly P\&I deaths for four US states. Light traces show the
  full time series; darker segments indicate the 20-week flu seasons
  (starting each November) included in the analysis. COVID-19 pandemic
  years (2020--2022) are excluded. A pre-season baseline is subtracted
  before model fitting.}
\label{fig:seasons}
\end{figure}

We estimated $\theta$ and its CRLB for all $\binom{47}{2} = 1081$
state pairs across 12~seasons. Figure~\ref{fig:theta_vs_crlb} shows
$\hat\theta$ against $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$---the lower bound on the
standard deviation of any unbiased estimator of $\theta$, which the
MLE attains asymptotically. The dashed line marks the 5\% one-sided
significance boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\%
confidence intervals that overlap zero. The overwhelming majority of
state pairs sit above the line, meaning that for nearly every pair of
US states the surveillance data provide no statistical evidence for
$\theta > 0$ under any unbiased estimator. The estimates
$\hat\theta$ themselves spread approximately uniformly over
$[0, 0.5]$: because the CRLB is large, each pair's point estimate is
driven by the particular noise realization in its 12~seasons rather
than by a signal in the data about coupling strength. The location of
$\hat\theta$ within $[0, 0.5]$ is therefore itself statistically
uninformative.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{../pix/theta_vs_crlb.pdf}
\caption{Estimated connectivity $\hat\theta$ vs.\ $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$
  for all 1081~US state pairs. The dashed line is the 5\% one-sided
  significance boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\%
  confidence intervals that overlap zero. Black circles:
  non-significant. Red stars: significant.}
\label{fig:theta_vs_crlb}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Entrainment Inflates the CRLB}

Figure~\ref{fig:crlb_ratio} shows the ratio of median CRLB
(synchronized / asynchronous) across a grid of connectivity values
$\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitudes $\delta$, with initial
conditions drawn from the empirical distribution of real-data fits
(SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). The ratio exceeds unity throughout the
parameter space, often by orders of magnitude: entrainment inflates
the minimum achievable estimation variance of the connectivity
parameter by factors that can exceed $10^3$ relative to the
asynchronous regime. This confirms, in a simulated setting with
realistic ICs, the entrainment-driven collapse of information about
$\theta$ seen in the real data.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/crlb_ratio.pdf}
\caption{Ratio of median CRLB (synchronized / asynchronous) as a
  function of connectivity $\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitude
  $\delta$. Initial conditions for each simulated season and region
  are drawn independently from the empirical distribution of
  per-season, per-state values fitted to the real data
  (SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). The ratio exceeds~1 throughout the
  parameter space.}
\label{fig:crlb_ratio}
\end{figure}

\subsection{AIC Prefers Multivariate, Forecast Skill Does Not}\label{subsec:var_results}

The time-series benchmark of \S\ref{subsec:varbench} reaches the same
conclusion as the CRLB analysis in a completely separate model class.
Across all 45 pairs among the ten largest US states, the bivariate
VAR is preferred on training-set AIC unanimously and by decisive
margins ($\Delta\text{AIC}$ median $-53$, range $[-161, -2]$,
negative values favouring the bivariate model). Yet the
corresponding out-of-sample test on 2023-2025 shows no systematic
forecast improvement: only 26 of 45 pairs (58\%) achieve lower
one-step-ahead RMSE with the bivariate model, the median RMSE change
from univariate to bivariate is a negligible $-0.87\%$, and the
distribution spans $[-6.9\%, +21.2\%]$. The bivariate model
\emph{hurts} forecasts for some pairs by more than twenty percent
while the best pair-wise improvement does not exceed seven percent
-- an asymmetry consistent with cross-lag coefficients picking up
noise that degrades out-of-sample performance rather than a
genuine predictive signal.

The divergence between AIC and forecast skill is diagnostic of
\emph{where} the multivariate statistical signal lives. Because
reduced-form VAR point forecasts depend only on past lags of both
series, the contemporaneous residual covariance $\Sigma_{12}$
affects the joint likelihood (hence AIC) but not the forecast mean;
the decomposition in eq.~\eqref{eq:varar_decomp} makes explicit that
AIC packages the $\Sigma_{12}$ contribution together with any
cross-lag predictive gain, while out-of-sample forecast skill sees
only the latter. Our observation that AIC prefers the bivariate
model by tens of units while test RMSE is essentially tied localises
the in-sample advantage to the $\Sigma_{12}$ term: states' weekly
mortality residuals are strongly contemporaneously correlated with
one another, but one state's past does not add useful information
about another state's future beyond what own-past plus shared
seasonality already provide.

This is the AR-framework analogue of our CRLB result, reached with a
separate model class (linear time-series rather than mechanistic
SIR) and a separate evaluation criterion (predictive accuracy on
held-out data rather than a Fisher-information lower bound). Both
analyses converge on the same operational recommendation. Cross-state
\emph{co-movement} is real, strong, and unavoidable; it is the
entrainment / shared-forcing signature our main analysis diagnoses.
Cross-state \emph{directed predictive structure}---the coupling
parameter~$\theta$ our CRLB argument targets---is not detectable by
either test. For seasonal-influenza preparedness forecasting, spatial
coupling carries no information the surveillance data can credibly
support and should not be fit as a free parameter.
