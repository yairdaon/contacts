\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Statistical Power Depends on Synchronization}

Figure~\ref{fig:power} shows the statistical power for detecting
non-zero connectivity $\theta$ as a function of the true connectivity
and seasonal amplitude, comparing synchronized (in-phase) and
asynchronous (out-of-phase) epidemics.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/power.pdf}
\caption{Statistical power for detecting connectivity $\theta > 0$ at
  5\% significance level. Left: synchronized epidemics (both regions
  in-phase). Right: asynchronous epidemics (regions out-of-phase by
  $\pi$). Rows correspond to $\log_{10}(\theta)$; columns to seasonal
  amplitude. Power is computed from the CRLB assuming an efficient
  estimator achieves the theoretical bound. Darker shading indicates
  higher power. When epidemics are synchronized, power remains low
  even for large $\theta$, indicating that connectivity cannot be
  reliably detected regardless of estimation method.}
\label{fig:power}
\end{figure}

The contrast is striking. For asynchronous epidemics (right panel),
power increases rapidly with $\theta$: connectivity values above
$\theta \approx 0.01$ can be reliably detected across all seasonal
amplitudes. In contrast, synchronized epidemics (left panel) show
uniformly low power across the entire parameter space. Even large
connectivity values ($\theta = 0.1$) cannot be reliably distinguished
from zero when both regions experience epidemics in phase.

\subsection*{CRLB Ratio}

To quantify the effect of synchronization on estimation precision, we
computed the ratio of median CRLB values between synchronized
(in-phase) and reverse-phase epidemics across all combinations of
connectivity $\theta$ and seasonal amplitude. Since the CRLB bounds
the variance of any unbiased estimator, this ratio directly indicates
how many times more samples would be required under synchronization to
achieve the same estimation precision as the reverse-phase case.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/crlb_ratio.pdf}
\caption{Ratio of median CRLB (in-phase / reverse-phase) as a function
  of connectivity $\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitude. The color
  scale is truncated at the 90th percentile to improve visibility.
  Values greater than 1 indicate that synchronized epidemics require
  more data to achieve the same estimation precision. Throughout nearly
  all of the parameter space, the ratio substantially exceeds 1,
  demonstrating that synchronization consistently degrades
  identifiability.}
\label{fig:crlb}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:crlb} reveals that across nearly the entire parameter
space, the CRLB ratio substantially exceeds 1. This means that
estimating connectivity from synchronized epidemics would require
correspondingly more data to match the precision achievable with
asynchronous epidemics. The effect is robust: regardless of the true
connectivity or seasonal amplitude, synchronization consistently
inflates the theoretical lower bound on estimation variance.

\subsection*{Likelihood Surface}

Figure~\ref{fig:likelihood} provides direct visualization of the
log-likelihood surface as a function of the connectivity parameter
$\theta$ for different phase offsets between regions. For each phase
difference $\varphi_1 - \varphi_2$, we generate synthetic data with
true connectivity $\theta = 0.05$ and evaluate the likelihood at the
true initial conditions across a grid of $\theta$ values.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../pix/likelihood_surface.pdf}
\caption{Log-likelihood as a function of connectivity $\theta$ for
  different phase offsets between regions. The vertical dashed line
  indicates the true value $\theta = 0.05$. For synchronized epidemics
  ($\varphi_1 - \varphi_2 = 0$), the likelihood is essentially flat
  across the entire range of $\theta$. As the phase difference
  increases, a clear peak emerges near the true value, confirming that
  asynchrony restores parameter identifiability.}
\label{fig:likelihood}
\end{figure}

For synchronized epidemics ($\varphi_1 - \varphi_2 = 0$), the
likelihood surface is nearly flat: changing $\theta$ from 0 to 0.5
produces negligible change in likelihood. This flatness directly
reflects the vanishing Fisher Information predicted by the
CRLB---when epidemics rise and fall together, the data contain
essentially no information about connectivity. As the phase difference
increases, a progressively sharper peak emerges near the true value
$\theta = 0.05$, confirming that phase asynchrony restores parameter
identifiability.

\subsection*{Real Data: P\&I Mortality}

We applied our methodology to pneumonia and influenza (P\&I) mortality
data from the United States. Figure~\ref{fig:raw_deaths} shows the
raw weekly P\&I death counts for four large states.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/raw_deaths.pdf}
\caption{Weekly P\&I death counts for California, New York, Texas, and
  Florida. The data exhibit clear seasonal peaks corresponding to
  winter influenza seasons, superimposed on a year-round baseline of
  non-influenza pneumonia deaths.}
\label{fig:raw_deaths}
\end{figure}

Since P\&I deaths include a substantial non-influenza baseline
(bacterial pneumonia, aspiration, other respiratory
infections~\citep{serfling1963}), we compute excess deaths by
subtracting the mean pre-season (July--October) death count from each
flu season. Figure~\ref{fig:seasons} shows the data with the flu
seasons used for inference highlighted.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/seasons_highlighted.pdf}
\caption{P\&I deaths for four states with flu seasons used for
  inference highlighted. Light shading shows the full time series;
  darker segments indicate the flu seasons (November through March)
  included in the analysis. COVID-19 pandemic years (2020--2022) are
  excluded.}
\label{fig:seasons}
\end{figure}

We estimated connectivity $\theta$ and its CRLB for all pairs among
the 20 largest US states. Figure~\ref{fig:theta_vs_crlb} shows
$\hat{\theta}$ against $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$ for each state pair. The
dashed line marks the significance boundary $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}} =
\hat{\theta}/1.96$: points above the line have 95\% confidence
intervals that include zero. Significant pairs (below the line) are
marked as red stars.

\begin{figure}%[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/theta_vs_crlb.pdf}
\caption{Estimated connectivity $\hat{\theta}$ vs.\ Cram\'er-Rao
  lower bound on its standard deviation for all pairs of the 20
  largest US states. The dashed line is the significance boundary:
  points above it cannot reject $\theta = 0$ at the 5\% level.
  Significant pairs are marked as red stars. The blue-shaded region
  indicates non-significance; the orange-shaded region indicates
  significance. Nearly all state pairs fall in the non-significant
  region, confirming that connectivity cannot be reliably inferred from
  synchronized P\&I mortality data.}
\label{fig:theta_vs_crlb}
\end{figure}
