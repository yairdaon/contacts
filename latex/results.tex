\section{Results}

\subsection{Entrainment Inflates the CRLB}
Figure~\ref{fig:crlb_ratio} shows the ratio of median CRLB
(synchronized / asynchronous) across a grid of connectivity values
$\theta$ and seasonal forcing amplitudes $\delta$, with initial
conditions drawn from the empirical distribution of real-data fits
(SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). The median ratio is $17.3$,
%% and the ratio exceeds unity in $97.9\%$ of cells, with sub-unity
%% values confined to the weakly-forced corner ($\delta \lesssim
%% 0.01$) and never falling below $0.78$.
the 95th-percentile ratio is $\sim 850$ and the maximum exceeds
$10^{5}$.  Entrainment therefore inflates the minimum achievable
estimation variance of the connectivity parameter by a typical factor
of order ten and by factors that can exceed $10^{3}$ throughout most
of the parameter space. These results confirm that entrainment
dominates synchronization in seasonal epidemics.

\caption{Ratio of median CRLB (synchronized / asynchronous) as a

  are drawn independently from the empirical distribution of
  per-season, per-state values fitted to the real data
  (SI~Fig.~\ref{fig:si_ics}). Median ratio is $17.3$; ratio~$>1$ in
  $98\%$ of cells.}

\subsection{Inter-Regional Connectivity in the US via Influenza}
We analyze excess pneumonia and influenza mortality from 47 US states
over 12 flu seasons.  Figure~\ref{fig:seasons} shows the raw weekly
death counts for four representative states, with the flu seasons used
for inference highlighted.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../pix/seasons_highlighted.pdf}
\caption{Weekly pneumonia and influenza deaths for four US
  states. Light traces show the full time series; darker segments
  indicate the 20-week flu seasons (starting each November) included
  in the analysis. COVID-19 pandemic years (2020--2022) are
  excluded.}
\label{fig:seasons}
\end{figure}

We estimated $\theta$ and its CRLB for all state pairs across
12~seasons. Figure~\ref{fig:theta_vs_crlb} shows $\hat\theta$ against
$\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$---the lower bound on the standard deviation of
any unbiased estimator of $\theta$, which the MLE attains
asymptotically. The dashed line marks the 5\% one-sided significance
boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\% confidence intervals
that overlap zero. The majority of state pairs are above the line; for
the vast majority of pairs of states the surveillance data
provide no statistical evidence for $\theta > 0$. The estimates
$\hat\theta$ themselves spread roughly uniformly over $[0, 0.5]$.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{../pix/theta_vs_crlb.pdf}
\caption{Estimated connectivity $\hat\theta$ vs.\ $\sqrt{\text{CRLB}}$
  for all US state pairs. The dashed line is the 5\% one-sided
  significance boundary: points above it have CRLB-derived 95\%
  confidence intervals that overlap zero. Black circles:
  non-significant. Red stars: significant.}
\label{fig:theta_vs_crlb}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Does a state covariate carry information beyond the national average?}\label{subsec:natavg_results}
On the (target, covariate) pairs for which all four SARIMA
configurations of \S\ref{subsec:sarimabench} converged, the lag-one
national average $\bar y_{t-1}$ alone reduces test-period RMSE in
$97\%$ of pairs (median $7.6\%$). We ask whether the covariate state's
lag-one mortality $y_{B,t-1}$ adds any information beyond it. It does
not: the relative RMSE change from adding $y_{B,t-1}$ on top of $\bar
y_{t-1}$ has a median of $+0.02\%$ and is negative (i.e.~improves
RMSE) in only $44\%$ of pairs (Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_density}). Once
the national-average covariate is in the model, the covariate state
contributes nothing.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../pix/sarima_density.pdf}
\caption{Distribution of relative test-period RMSE change
  (eq.~\eqref{eq:sarima_delta}) when the covariate state's lag-one
  mortality $y_{B,t-1}$ is added as a second covariate to a SARIMA
  that already includes the lag-one national average $\bar y_{t-1}$
  across (target, covariate) pairs. Negative values mean adding the
  covariate state improves the forecast. The distribution is nearly
  symmetric around zero (median $+0.02\%$, dashed red line), showing
  that the covariate state carries no information beyond the national
  average.}
\label{fig:sarima_density}
\end{figure}

This is exactly the prediction of mechanism (ii) of
\S\ref{subsec:sarimabench}: the modest effect visible in
Fig.~\ref{fig:sarima_heatmap} essentially disappears. The information
in $y_{B,t-1}$ is merely a proxy for the national-level severity, which
the national average captures cleanly. Under (i), $y_{B,t-1}$ would
carry information not contained in $\bar y_{t-1}$ and the joint model
would beat the national-average-only model. We see no such effect.
