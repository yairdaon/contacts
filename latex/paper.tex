\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Significance Statement.}
Estimating how infectious diseases spread between regions from
outbreak data is a natural goal, but our analysis shows it can produce
misleading results.  When regional epidemics are synchronized---as
influenza is across the United States each winter---a standard
maximum-likelihood fit will return a connectivity estimate that looks
reasonable yet has a confidence interval spanning nearly the entire
parameter space.  We derive a theoretical bound showing exactly when
this happens: synchronization combined with similar initial epidemic
conditions destroys the information needed to identify coupling.  Any
asymmetry between regions---different epidemic timing or different
starting immunity---restores identifiability.  These results extend to
any system of coupled oscillators driven by common forcing, including
neural circuits and ecological networks.
